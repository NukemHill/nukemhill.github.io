Every Complex System Has a Shape

On chaos, abstraction, relationships, and the work of making complexity navigable without erasing what matters.

Years ago I became fascinated with chaos theory. At first, I thought I was studying randomness. Instead, I discovered structure.

Chaos theory is often described as the study of unpredictable systems, but that description misses what fascinated me most. Beneath behavior that appeared impossibly complicated lay relationships of remarkable consistency. Tiny changes could produce dramatically different outcomes, yet those outcomes were never arbitrary. They emerged from systems governed by understandable rules.

That realization stayed with me long after I stopped reading books on chaos theory.

Over the years I began encountering the same phenomenon in places that, at first glance, seemed to have nothing in common. Software systems, governments, scientific knowledge, organizations, and, more recently, artificial intelligence all shared a curious characteristic. Each appeared overwhelmingly complex until viewed from the right perspective. Once that perspective emerged, what had looked like chaos began to organize itself into something coherent.

The complexity had not disappeared. Only my understanding of it had changed.

I eventually realized that nearly every project I found meaningful began with the same question—not How should I build this?, but What is the shape of the system I’m trying to understand?

That question has quietly guided much of my career. Whether designing software, modeling legislative processes, exploring knowledge graphs, or developing languages for AI systems, I have found myself searching for the same thing: the abstraction that reveals the underlying structure without sacrificing the richness of the original system.

For many people, the word abstraction implies simplification. We imagine removing details until only the essentials remain. I’ve come to think of it differently.

A good abstraction does not make a system smaller. It makes it understandable.

The difference matters. Simplification often succeeds by discarding information. A useful abstraction succeeds by preserving what is essential while revealing the relationships that give the system its form. It reduces the mental effort required to reason about a problem without reducing the fidelity of the model itself.

In that sense, abstraction is not the opposite of complexity. It is the means by which complexity becomes navigable.

This is one of the reasons graph-based thinking has always appealed to me. Graphs begin with relationships rather than categories. They do not force structure onto a system; they allow structure to emerge naturally from the connections that already exist. As those relationships accumulate, the shape of the system gradually reveals itself.

I’ve come to believe that the same principle extends far beyond databases. Governments are networks of relationships. Scientific knowledge is a network of relationships. Organizations, software architectures, and even human ideas derive much of their meaning not from their individual components, but from the connections between them.

The work of engineering, then, is not merely to construct systems. It is to discover representations that preserve those relationships while making them visible.

Every project collected in this notebook is an exploration of that idea. CivicSignal seeks the shape of government. ProjectOS explores the shape of AI collaboration. JournalNexus investigates the shape of scholarly knowledge. Other projects examine the evolution of human thought itself.

The domains are different. The pursuit is the same.

Every complex system has a shape.

The work of the architect is to discover it. The work of software is to reveal it.
