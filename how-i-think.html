Working philosophy

How I Think

The hardest problems in software are usually problems of representation before they are problems of implementation.

I have spent much of my career discovering that the code is rarely the first hard part. The first hard part is understanding what the system actually is.

Complex domains present themselves as noise: requirements, exceptions, institutions, data formats, human habits, historical accidents, and rules that only make sense in relation to other rules. The instinct is often to simplify aggressively. I prefer to look for the structure that allows the complexity to remain intact while becoming navigable.

What is the shape of the system, and what representation will let us see it?

Abstraction without erasure

A useful abstraction is not merely a smaller version of reality. It is a representation that preserves the features and relationships necessary for the work at hand. The best abstractions reduce cognitive load without quietly destroying meaning.

That distinction explains why I am drawn to database schemas, intermediate representations, compilers, graphs, protocols, and domain languages. Each is a way to make structure explicit. Each can also become dangerous when the representation is mistaken for the whole system.

Relationships before categories

Graph thinking has been a recurring influence because graphs begin with relationships. A hierarchy asks where something belongs. A graph asks what it connects to, how, and why. In domains such as government, knowledge, organizations, and AI coordination, the relationships often carry more meaning than any single object.

Questions before solutions

I try to delay implementation certainty long enough to ask better questions. What remains invariant? What changes by state, customer, or environment? Which source is authoritative? What evidence would prove success? What information must survive a handoff? What is reversible, and what creates durable consequences?

Once those questions are answered, architecture becomes less mysterious. Components, boundaries, state, and interfaces begin to follow from the model.

Determinism where it matters

Human conversation is fluid, ambiguous, and generative. Execution cannot be. My recent AI systems work separates natural-language intent from the deterministic contracts required to mutate durable state. The goal is not to remove human language, but to compile it into inspectable representations with bounded authority, integrity checks, and evidence.

One inquiry, several domains

CivicSignal seeks the shape of government. ProjectOS seeks the shape of coordinated AI work. POSL seeks the shape of executable intent. JournalNexus asks about the shape of scholarly discovery. Project Philosophia asks how questions and ideas shaped the history of human thinking.

They are different projects, but not separate intellectual pursuits. They are variations of one continuing question: how can software reveal the structure of a complex system without flattening the qualities that make the system worth understanding?

My working rule: preserve provenance, expose relationships, separate truth from projection, and make every important transition inspectable.
