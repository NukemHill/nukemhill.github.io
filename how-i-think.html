How I Think

The hardest problems in software are rarely about code. They are about understanding.

Software has always fascinated me, but not because I enjoy writing code for its own sake.

What interests me is understanding.

Every meaningful project I have worked on—from enterprise software and mobile applications to civic technology and artificial intelligence—has eventually become the same exercise:

How do we make a complex system understandable?

I do not believe complexity is something to be feared. Most complex systems are simply waiting for the right abstraction.

Sometimes that abstraction is a database schema. Sometimes it is an intermediate representation. Sometimes it is a compiler. Sometimes it is a graph. Sometimes it is a new language.

Finding those abstractions is the work I enjoy most. Everything else follows.

The questions stay the same

Over the years I have worked in mobile software, enterprise systems, distributed applications, databases, consulting, and product architecture. The technologies have changed dramatically. The underlying questions have not.

  • How do people reason about complicated systems?
  • How do we organize information?
  • How do we communicate intent?
  • How do we make software easier to understand?

Those questions eventually led me to explore AI systems, compiler design, civic intelligence, and knowledge architecture. Although the domains are different, they are all attempts to solve the same problem.

Different projects, one through-line

ProjectOS explores deterministic execution for AI systems. CivicSignal explores how software can help citizens understand government. Signal Alliance explores how specialized AI systems can collaborate on complex engineering work. JournalNexus is an early conceptual exploration of how AI may reshape scholarly discovery.

At first glance, these projects may seem unrelated. To me, they are all chapters of the same book.

I do not think software should merely automate complexity. I think it should reveal structure.

When software exposes the relationships hidden inside a system, people make better decisions, ask better questions, and build better tools.

That is the kind of software I hope to spend the rest of my career building.
