The through-line

Chaos theory first taught me that apparent disorder can still contain persistent form. Graphs taught me to look for relationships before categories. Compilers taught me to separate human intent from executable representation. Civic systems taught me that simplification is dangerous when it destroys meaning.

Together, those lessons have become a working architectural principle: a useful abstraction does not merely remove detail. It preserves the features and relationships that matter while reducing the effort required to understand and act.

Good software does not eliminate complexity. It reveals the order that already exists inside it.
